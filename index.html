Nametag
Customizing My Nametag

Customizing a Nametag

Workflow Steps

I downloaded a Nametag 3MF file from Brandeis's Public Workshop, opened it in PrusaSlicer, and put in my name. I changed the font as well!

Many thanks to everyone who contributes to engineering and maker resources on campus!

Getting Ready

I adjusted the settings in the upper-right corner according to the printer and filament used in the project. After clicking "Slice Now" in the bottom right corner, the anatomy of my print showed up. You can see the texture of this upcoming print.

Then I click "Export G-Code" and save the file to a U-drive. Check the print bed and the filament, and plug in the U-drive. It's time for the printer to start working!

Comparison

Font Matters: Compared to the font I used at that time, the font I'm using this time was printed more coarsely.

Calibration (“torture”) print
The Puking Cubic Cat

The Puking Cubic Cat

It's not supposed to puke...Oops!

When I opened the STL file, I noticed the cat's tail is tilted at an angle, which seemed large to me. I started wondering: Will the project fail if I don't generate support for it, as it is called a "torture" print? So I tried generating organic support. It seemed a bit too ORGANIC.

Print Quality and More Observations

Dimensional Accuracy? This cat is pretty accurate! For more complicated designs, which include multiple portions and need to be assembled, dimensional accuracy plays a more important role. By slightly reducing the volume of a print, the time consumed shrinks in a cubic exponent.

I didn't choose to print a huge cat. It's small, condensed, and of good quality. I tried to squeeze it and felt its firmness: 15% to 25% filament indeed is enough for most prints.

I used pliers to take the support off. The space between the print and the support is slightly larger, making it possible to do the removal. What an unnecessary amount of work!

Limits of 3D Printing

Although this project does not require support, some prints do. In some cases, there might be overhangs, for the filament was not supported when it was extruded, and started to fall due to gravity.

Compared to the Nametag print, which was a 3MF file, the STL file seemed simpler. It consists of a geometrical design, but no detailed information. Meanwhile, a 3MF file includes information about colors, textures, materials, and print settings. There might be multiple parts in one 3MF file.

Open-source assistive devices
Braille Business Card Slate and Stylus Set

Braille Business Card Slate and Stylus Set

The Print

is from @Makers Making Change-https://www.makersmakingchange.com/product/braille-business-card-slate-and-stylus-set/01tJR0000006996YAA

Why This Print?

As the name indicates, this is a tool designed for writing Braille on standard-sized business cards. This kit enables people with visual impairments to create Braille on standard business cards. Just like a lot of other designs on Makers Making Change, this is a set that helps people in the minority. Some other designs, like milk carton openers or key holders, deal with minor issues in daily life. Some of the projects might not be suitable or appealing enough for mass manufacture, as there might not be a high demand. 3D printing, however, makes these little sparks come true. And due to its flexibility, customization is also possible.

Concerns: The kit consists of three little parts. I almost lost one of them because they are all small and light. Maybe we could update the kit by intentionally leaving a hole in the corner of each piece, thus providing users with an option to tie the kit together with a string.

Flaws? Why?

As the picture shows, one of the pieces was not printed perfectly, so I gave it another try. It turned out slightly better.

The flaws could be caused by: Overheating? Extruder operating too fast? I shall figure it out in later projects.

CAD Orientation
Designing an Emoji Ring in TinkerCad V.S. in Fusion

Designing an Emoji Ring in TinkerCad V.S. in Fusion

In TinkerCad

Tinkercad is a suitable choice for beginners, as it provides fun models that can be easily combined. I made a ring shape by doing subtraction with two cylinders with different diameters. Then I combined it with an Emoji, an existing model.

In Fusion

This process is much more complicated in Fusion.

Fusion enables designers to create shapes on a 2D plane and then extrude them into 3D models. This means Fusion can be used to build more complicated designs. It does not have funny, already-built models like Tinkercad. As a result, making an Emoji in Fusion means doing subtraction, aka two holes, on a sphere.

Tinkercad Remix
Toad's Treasure Box

Tinkercad Remix - Toad's Treasure Box

Sketching a Draft

I am immersed in the lovely models TinkerCad provided! I want to print a toad, for it is such a complex shape that I wonder how much support it would require. I chose to eliminate the key later, since I only want to make a small print, and it could, as a result, be very fragile.

Design Process

The alignment tool in TinkerCad helps a lot. Since this is a 3D design, adjusting the models manually can be a bit challenging. By using Alignment, I quickly adjusted the toad to a desired position.

However, I forgot the measures of both parts. And turned out that the body and the lid do not match perfectly.

Printing and Making

Before I started printing, I changed my mind about the color choice. I made one version with a colorful filament, and a second one by slicing my model and changing the filament halfway through the printing process.

It took me a long time to remove the supports under the toad. I'm happy with my prints, though. If I print it a third time, I would be more careful with the measures so that the lid could match the body.

Fusion Remix
Pulling up a Box from Scratch!

Fusion Remix - Oval Comtainer with Stars

Sketching a Draft

As for the box, I drew an oval on the X-Z plane, pulled it up into a three-dimensional shape, and made another oval-cylindrical shape as an extraction. As for the star-shaped decoration, I drew a star, pulled it into a 3D version, and rounded its corners.

Oops Failed Prints

The print failed catastrophically. Twice. Heartbreaking.

It turned out to be a problem with my design. I did not double-check my models before I exported them from Fusion. Some of the models were slightly below the plane, thus leading to the failures. I later fixed the problem by lifting the models above the plane.

After two epic fails, I decided to print a much smaller version of my design, so that I can save some time and readjust quickly if there are still errors. I learned how to reduce the size of my models. Unlike pressing the shift key like I did in TinkerCad, I found the option in the toolbar in Fusion. This is why the print turned out slightly larger than a fingernail. It still took about 20 minutes, though.

Fusion is so much more complicated!

This print turned out exactly how I want, with a lid that can fall inside, a little quirky. I know this is a simple combination of simple shapes. Wish I could make something more complicated!

I would like to make a little drawer, cabinet, or something with several components. It is going to be a lot more designing.
