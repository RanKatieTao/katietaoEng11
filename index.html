Customizing a Nametag

Customizing a Nametag

Workflow Steps

I downloaded a Nametag 3MF file from Brandeis's Public Workshop, opened it in PrusaSlicer, and put in my name. I changed the font as well!

Many thanks to everyone who contributes to engineering and maker resources on campus!

Getting Ready

I adjusted the settings in the upper-right corner according to the printer and filament used in the project. After clicking "Slice Now" in the bottom right corner, the anatomy of my print showed up. You can see the texture of this upcoming print!

Then I click "Export G-Code" and save the file to a U-drive. Check the print bed and the filament, and plug in the U-drive. It's time for the printer to start working!

Comparison

I printed a nametag the semester before in an English class about medieval studies and maker culture. It was in a different font!

Compared to the font I used at that time, the font I'm using this time was printed more coarsely.

The Puking Cubic Cat

The Puking Cubic Cat

It's not supposed to puke...Oops!

When I opened the STL file, I noticed the cat's tail is tilted at an angle, which seemed large to me. I started wondering: Will the project fail if I don't generate support for it, as it is called a "torture" print? So I tried generating organic support. It seemed a bit too ORGANIC.

Print Quality and More Observations

By slightly reducing the volume of a print, the time consumed shrinks in a cubic exponent.

I didn't choose to print a huge cat. It's small, condensed, and of good quality. I tried to squeeze it and felt its firmness: 15% to 25% filament indeed is enough for most prints.

I used pliers to take the support off. The space between the print and the support is slightly larger, making it possible to do the removal. What an unnecessary amount of work!

Limits of 3D Printing

Although this project does not require support, some prints do. In some cases, there might be overhangs, for the filament was not supported when it was extruded, and started to fall due to gravity.

Compared to the Nametag print, which was a 3MF file, the STL file seemed simpler. It consists of a geometrical design, but no detailed information.

Braille Business Card Slate and Stylus Set

Braille Business Card Slate and Stylus Set

It's not supposed to puke...Oops!

The model is from @Makers Making Change-https://www.makersmakingchange.com/product/braille-business-card-slate-and-stylus-set/01tJR0000006996YAA

Why This Print?

As the name indicates, this is a tool designed for writing Braille on standard-sized business cards. This kit enables people with visual impairments to create Braille on standard business cards. Just like a lot of other designs on Makers Making Change, this is a set that helps people in the minority. Some other designs, like milk carton openers or key holders, deal with minor issues in daily life. Some of the projects might not be suitable or appealing enough for mass manufacture, as there might not be a high demand. 3D printing, however, makes these little sparks come true. And due to its flexibility, customization is also possible.

Concerns: The kit consists of three little parts. I almost lost one of them because they are all small and light. Maybe we could update the kit by intentionally leaving a hole in the corner of each piece, thus providing users with an option to tie the kit together with a string.
